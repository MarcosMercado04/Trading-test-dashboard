The honest journey

Most dashboards show you the best number. This one shows how that number survived contact with reality — Sharpe ratio at each stage of validation.

The lesson the data taught us: a hyper-tuned single config is curve-fit (Sharpe 0.92 → −0.05 on unseen data). But the underlying structure, held fixed and applied to a brand-new instrument, holds up — strongly on Nasdaq, weakly on the Dow. Adding an early-week filter lifts every instrument further and survives the out-of-sample window — but day-of-week effects are the easiest thing in markets to overfit, so we carry that number with a healthy dose of suspicion (see the caveat below).

What the internet claims vs. what we measured

Opening-range breakout is one of the most-hyped retail strategies. Before trusting any of it, we tested the popular claims against five years of real data. Most don't hold up.

Didn't replicate
The 5-minute opening range is best.
In our tests the 5-minute range was the worst setting — essentially break-even. Longer ranges were materially better. The headline claim simply didn't replicate.
Overstated
ORB delivers huge returns.
After realistic costs the edge is modest but real — not the triple-digit headlines. Those come from heavy leverage, options, or cherry-picked windows, not the base strategy.
Partly false
Trade it symmetrically, long and short.
Over 2021–2026 the short side eroded returns; long-only was the better risk-adjusted strategy. It's period-dependent — a sustained downtrend could flip it.
Misleading
It wins most of the time.
Win rates sat around half. The edge comes from average win > average loss — letting winners run while a stop caps losers — not from a high hit rate.
False
Costs and slippage barely matter.
With profit factors near 1.1–1.2, costs are decisive. Double the slippage and the naive version goes negative. They must always be modelled.
Holds up
Let winners run.
The one idea that survived: a simple end-of-day exit (no tight cap) beat most fancier exits. Breakouts pay off when the big trends aren't cut short.

How we pressure-tested it

A backtest that only shows its best number is marketing. Every result here had to survive a four-stage gauntlet designed to expose overfitting.

Grid search

Swept 900+ configurations — timeframes, range lengths, direction, exits, filters and entry cutoffs — across five years.

→ Best in-sample Sharpe ≈ 0.9. Tempting — and exactly the kind of number to distrust.

Holdout test

Locked that single best config, then ran it once on recent data it had never been optimised on.

→ It collapsed to ≈ 0. The "winner" was curve-fit to the sample.

Walk-forward

Rolled a train/test window forward, re-selecting fresh each step — the honest estimate when you re-optimise as you go.

→ Realistic Sharpe far below the headline, and negative in several windows.

Cross-instrument

Fixed the structure — not the dialled-in parameters — and applied it unchanged to indices it was never tuned on.

→ Strong on Nasdaq, solid on the S&P, weak on the Dow. A real but momentum-dependent edge.
We got it wrong first — and fixed it. Our initial cross-instrument test let the optimiser re-pick the best config on every fold. That just re-overfits each window, and it told us the edge "didn't replicate." The flaw was in the test, not the strategy: the right way is to fix the hypothesis and apply it unchanged. Correcting the methodology flipped the conclusion. We show both — because the mistake is the most instructive part of the whole project.

Enhancements we tried — and mostly rejected

Every popular "improvement" — plus a couple we explored ourselves — tested across the grid. Most didn't survive out-of-sample. Exact winning settings are withheld; the direction of each finding isn't.

Profit targetsMostly no

Tight targets raised the win rate but gutted profit — they cut the big winners that pay for the losers. Only a very wide target marginally helped.

Trailing stopsSometimes

Helped only when the trail distance matched the range width. Applied blindly, it didn't beat a simple end-of-day exit.

Trend filterRejected

Filtering by the prior-day close hurt on average and didn't improve the best setups — long-only already captures the bias it was meant to add.

Entry-time cutoffKept

The single biggest improvement: restricting entries to earlier in the session roughly doubled Sharpe and cut drawdown. Morning breakouts have all day to trend into the close.

Day-of-week filterOptional · caution

Concentrating entries in the early part of the week roughly doubled Sharpe and cut drawdown, and — unusually — held up out-of-sample on all three indices, so we wired it in as a toggle above (Full week ↔ Early week). The warning we keep front-of-mind: the single best in-sample weekday became one of the worst out-of-sample. Day-of-week is the easiest thing in markets to overfit — flip the toggle and judge for yourself.

Long vs shortConfirmed

Splitting a both-sides run by direction: longs carried the entire edge; shorts diluted it on every index and lost money on the least momentum-driven one. Exactly why the structure is long-only.

Taking it to a prop account

Futures prop firms fund traders who pass an evaluation. The rule that busts most accounts isn't the profit target — it's the maximum drawdown. We Monte-Carlo'd the strategy against it: 20,000 simulated evaluations per setup, bootstrapped from its own trades, sweeping position size and the funded phase.

The headline: micros on a trailing-drawdown account, or any mini on a static-drawdown account. Trailing-DD firms punish big single-trade swings, so the minis (NQ/YM) sit borderline there — but Tradeify Lightning's static drawdown (the floor never trails up) flips them to viable.

How to use it successfully

Trade micros on trailing accounts

On Topstep / Tradeify Advanced, MES micros let you size so a single losing trade can't blow the trailing floor. That's what makes them the only consistently viable trailing-DD combo.

Use static drawdown for minis

Want NQ or YM size? A static-drawdown account (Tradeify Lightning) doesn't trail your floor upward, so a normal pullback after profit won't end the account — minis become viable.

Keep the early-week filter on

Early-week trading materially lifts pass odds versus full-week across every firm and instrument — fewer, higher-quality trades clear the target with less drawdown exposure.

Size for the floor, not the target

≈3 micros per $50K trailing account is the sweet spot. Scale up only as your realised buffer grows — chasing the target with size is what trips the drawdown.

Respect the funded phase

Passing is one hurdle; keeping the account is another. Bust risk compounds over a year, so trade the funded account more conservatively than the eval.

How it works & what to distrust

Structure · exact parameters withheld

  • Opening range = high/low of the first N minutes after the 09:30 ET cash open (length tuned & withheld).
  • Direction — long-only: enter when price breaks and closes beyond the range.
  • Risk — stop on the opposite side of the range; profit target at a multiple of that risk (multiple withheld).
  • Timing — morning-session entries, with an optional early-week filter (toggle above); otherwise flat by the 16:00 ET close.
  • Costs modelled per instrument (commission + 2 ticks slippage).

Honest caveats

  • The optional day-of-week filter (toggle above) lifts results and held out-of-sample here — but day-of-week effects are notoriously fragile; treat that uplift with suspicion.
  • Structure was selected on MES over this window — NQ/YM are the cleaner reads.
  • Long-only in a mostly-rising market — some edge is index drift (2022 was down and still positive, which helps the case).
  • 2024–25 was the weak stretch across all three — not stable in every regime.
  • Single contract, simple fills, continuous .c.0 roll, no overnight.
  • A true forward-in-time test on new data is still the gold standard.
